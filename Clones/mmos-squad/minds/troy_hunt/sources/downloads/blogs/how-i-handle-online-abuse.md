# Here's How I Handle Online Abuse
**Date:** October 17, 2016
**Source:** https://www.troyhunt.com/heres-how-i-handle-online-abuse/
**Category:** Ethics & Values

Troy Hunt shares his personal approach to managing online harassment and negative comments from antagonists. He explains what constitutes abuse versus constructive disagreement, addresses common grievances directed at him, and outlines practical strategies for handling hostile behavior.

## What Hunt Considers Abuse

Hunt distinguishes between disagreement and abuse. While he welcomes "vehement disagreement" and "constructive argument," he categorizes as abuse comments designed primarily to "insult or cause harm," particularly when delivered anonymously. He acknowledges that his experiences pale compared to serious harassment campaigns involving threats to safety or family.

He emphasizes giving hundreds of talks with minimal direct compensation, framing these as investments in exposure and networking rather than profit opportunities.

**Teaching style criticism:** He defends explaining security concepts to developers using accessible tools like WiFi Pineapple. His strategy targets software developers specifically, not security specialists, prioritizing comprehension over academic rigor for his primary audience.

**Tall poppy syndrome:** Hunt references the Australian cultural term describing "resentment or envy of the success of a peer." He observes that positive announcements frequently attract disproportionately hostile responses from isolated detractors.

**Corporate affiliation accusations:** Hunt maintains he became a Microsoft Regional Director and MVP through years of unpaid advocacy before receiving any benefits. He purchased his own equipment and refuses endorsements inconsistent with his independent judgment.

## His Response Strategy

Hunt evolved from confrontation toward strategic disengagement. He now employs:

- **Muting** on Twitter rather than blocking, preventing visibility of dismissal
- **Self-muting** by avoiding revisiting hostile discussions
- **Blog comment deletion** for abusive posts while preserving critical discourse
- **Ignoring bravado** rooted in anonymity, recognizing rational conversation is unlikely

A turning point came observing Robert Scoble's graceful response to a nasty comment, which enhanced Scoble's reputation while diminishing the critic's credibility.

## The Underlying Philosophy

Hunt poses a critical question to antagonists: "Would you say this face-to-face at a conference?" Behavior rooted in anonymity often disappears under personal accountability.

Rather than combating negativity directly, Hunt emphasizes producing quality work. He references an observed principle: "You can't remove all negative things about you from the internet, the best thing you can do is to flood the web with positive things."

His final recommendation: ignore abuse unless genuinely unavoidable, recognizing antagonists represent "a small fraction of 1% of people" he interacts with online.
